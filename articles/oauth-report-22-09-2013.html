The OAuth Report #1 : Social logins

tl;dr : On early-stage apps, Facebook connect permissions are accepted only by 70,94% of visitors, Google+ connect ones by 88,81%, Linkedin connect 57,53%, Github connect 49.77% ant Twitter connect 60,22%

Login registration forms are known to not be efficient enough, since many people provide false data, forget their login information for the site or simply decline to register in the first place.

Social connect is a single sign-on process that uses existing login information from an existing social networking service such as Facebook, Twitter or Google+ to sign into a third-party website in lieu of creating a new login account specifically for that website. It is designed to simplify logins for end users as well as provide more and more reliable user information to web developers and do not have to store passwords.

Social login is a gateway for authentication and authorization, according to the Wikipedia definiton ,

For consumer websites that offer social functionalities to users, social login is often implemented using the OAuth standard. OAuth is a secure authorization protocol which is commonly used in conjunction with authentication to grant 3rd party applications a "session token" allowing them to make API calls to providers on the user's behalf. Sites using social login in this manner typically offer social features such as commenting, sharing, reactions and gamification (1).

It is important to stress that "the user has to explicitly grant the 3rd-party access to the social provider".

Understanding the context of the report

OAuth.io is an OAuth-as-a-Service enabling developers to consume OAuth from dozens of providers for the authentication or authorization of their application. We took advantage of OAuth.io to make a study on end-user behavior testing registration with different social login providers. The study was done on 20,000 end users of a total of 1,370 early-stage applications.

These results are for the moment only valid for early-stage applications that have not gained massive traction of users yet.

However, we intend to release a monthly report that could help our developers improve their registration flow. As their user base grows, the results of the report could become more generally relevant.

Social login acceptance tested with Facebook, Twitter, Google+, LinkedIn and Github

When a user clicks on a social login button, the user is asked to grant the 3rd-party application access to their account on the corresponding social network:

"Do you authorize this application to access your account?". Some users accept, others don't.

Here is some important vocabulary we defined, that is necessary for understanding the study on social logins:

Accept: the user clicked on the social login button and accepted to log in, with the social provider, to the 3rd party application. Please note, that the user might have denied access to all or some permissions.

Deny: the user clicked on the social login button but when faced with the permissions requested by the 3rd party application, the user denied access.

Facebook connect: 70.94% accept and 29.06% deny

Facebook is the most used social login, with more than 7 million Facebook connect distributed on websites and applications (3)

Our study was made on 1370 apps using Facebook connects during 20 days , from the 07/31/2013 to the 08/29/2013.

So it seems that for early stage applications, Facebook connect is not as effective as we could think, as only nearly 71% accept Facebook permissions from the application.

People may be very vigilant with their Identity management on the web, and permissions asked may make them refuse to login further as they were.

In a further study, we will publish also the permissions that make a Facebook connect more or less accepted or denied. These numbers just show that a Facebook connect is not the magic solution, you have also to optimize it with your user experience requirements and your users acceptance.

What are the possible interpretations?

- It depends on the level of permissions required. A Facebook Connect asking only to see you friends' list and another one asking to post on your wall without your approval will not have the same acceptance level. Try to discover a maximum who are your users to avoid them to stop the registration process just for a permission that goes too far. Or smarter, ask small permissions in the registration and ask more in-app permissions. We will publish in the next months statistics depending with the different permissions. (Technically called scope for OAuth 2.0)

- It depends on the privacy management and the profile of the social network users. It seems that Google+ users are more likely to accept social login than others. It can be due to the average profile of Google+ users than Facebook, Twitter or LinkedIn more aware about privacy and how to manage it for registering to applications. Github seems very low, maybe be because developers are more don't want to share easily

- It depends on the final end user application We made a sample of 1,370 applications early stage and 20,000 end users, we do not represent all the numbers of mass users applications. We can imagine that a trendy application like Spotify or Airbnb is more likely to have its social login process accepted because of the traction and the social proof of the service.
